# The Eleventh Hour

He took one last sip of his coffee. It had gone cold an hour ago—everything in the laboratory had—but he drank it anyway, the way a condemned man eats his final meal slowly, to make the world last a little longer.

Then he set the cup down on the steel table, leaned his weight onto his boot, and pressed once more, hard, on the right hand of the mercenary lying on the floor.

The man howled.

Muatti held his own left arm against his chest with his right, cradling it the way you cradle something already lost. Half the arm was gone to ribbons—the bullets had chewed through muscle and nerve until he could barely feel his fingers. He should have been screaming too. He wasn't. Somewhere in the last twenty minutes the fear had drained out of him completely, the way his blood was draining out of him now, and what it left behind was a terrible, weightless calm.

"Scream," Muatti said softly. "Louder. Go on." He pressed harder. "Maybe someone who has news of me will hear you. Maybe they'll come."

They wouldn't. No one came to this wing of **St. Jude's** after dark. That was the entire point of it.

At the far end of the table sat the other one—slumped in a chair, very still, a kitchen knife buried to the handle in the center of his forehead. Muatti had put it there himself, though he could no longer remember the act with any clarity. The man's dead eyes were fixed on the ceiling. *Right where the mark would be,* Muatti thought, with the detachment of the half-dead. *Right where the rose blooms.*

"Let me go," the man on the floor wept. "Please. For God's sake, let me go."

Muatti said nothing. The screaming had stopped frightening him. It had become almost gentle—a lullaby, rising and falling, rocking him toward the edge of something. His eyes drifted to the middle distance, to the swimming dark beyond the lamplight.

And then the worst thing began to happen. The thing every dying man is warned about.

His life began to play, frame by frame, like a reel of film.

Muatti had never liked his life. He watched it now with a stranger's distaste. But for the first time in as long as he could remember, he felt longing—a clean, aching homesickness for years and years ago. For the time before he had ever heard the words **Project Glacier**.

---

He had been a hard boy. He had grown up in the alleys, raised on street fights, and no one could put him down for long. The older boys had beaten the skill into him one bruise at a time, until the day came when he could beat them back—and then beat them first. He had spent his whole youth clawing his way up out of that gutter, and he had told himself he'd left it behind for good.

But the gutter, it turned out, was not the deepest place a man could fall.

Every time his mind tried to drift somewhere warm, it snapped back to the same cold room. The hours in the laboratory. Even dying, there was no climbing out of Project Glacier.

He had been young when it took him—fresh from his examinations, a clever, money-hungry boy in a clean white coat. He'd had no cause, no banner, no great love for his fellow man. He'd wanted a salary, a title, and a name people said with respect. Why not? *Not everyone needs to be a hero,* he had told himself, and he had believed it the way you believe things that are convenient.

When the offer came from St. Jude's, he took it for the money and stayed for the way they looked at him there—as if he mattered. The pay was extraordinary, because no one in their right mind wanted to work behind those white walls. He told himself he was healing people. Some of it was even true.

But he was afraid. There were patients in the restricted wing who were *special*, and a word kept drifting down the corridors like a draft under a door—**RedLink**. He didn't know what it meant. He knew only that it was a secret with weight, that the men who guarded it never smiled, and that the one time he opened his mouth to ask, his own voice frightened him back into silence.

He worked there three years before the thing in the restricted wing reached out and touched his superior.

**Doctor Crale** had been a brilliant man—precise, ambitious, untouchable. Then, across a single winter, he came apart. He stopped sleeping. He stood at the observation glass for hours with his palms flat against it. He began to speak in a low, even voice about *twelve hearts beating like one drum*, about *a black sun under the mountain*, about being submerged in the memory of a people who had died before the city was ever built. They took him away one morning with his pupils blown out into two black coins, fixed on something none of the rest of them could see.

That was the day they promoted Muatti. They needed someone for the **Touch of Rose** patients, and Muatti had the one qualification that mattered: he was still sane, and he still wanted to be paid.

---

The salary they gave him then was obscene. He could see the house at the end of it. The car. The whole gleaming life he'd been climbing toward since the alleys.

So he went down into the ward, and he sat with them—the Rose-Marked, the ones the city had given up on. Children, mostly. They lay in their circle of beds with the crimson leaf burning faintly on their foreheads and their eyes fixed on a horizon that was not in the room. They did not breathe the way the living breathe. He ran his tests. He took his measurements. He tried, year after year, to understand what was wrong with them.

And slowly—the way a man finally hears a clock he has long stopped noticing—he began to hear the rhythm.

It came every eleven hours. Exactly eleven. He thought at first it was a fault in his instruments: the way the readings died, the way the air thickened and the marks flared brighter and then dimmed, like a held breath let out. But it was no fault. It was a pulse. A heartbeat that belonged to no single one of them and to all of them at once. He charted it. He timed it against the clock on the wall until there could be no doubt left in him.

Eleven hours. Muatti found the eleven hours. He set it down on paper in his own careful hand, and when he understood what he had written, he was more afraid than he had ever been in any alley in his life.

Because the others believed the patients were *broadcasting*. Corshield's reports—the ones Muatti was never meant to read—spoke of an *antenna*, a *window*, an instrument. But Muatti had sat with them in the dark for years, and he had learned the truth the reports had gotten backwards. The Rose-Marked were not transmitters.

They were *answering*.

Something beneath the mountain was calling, once every eleven hours, patient as a glacier, and the children in that ward were turning their faces toward it the way flowers turn toward a sun. He had not opened a window. He had found a door. And on the other side of it waited a power he had no words for and no business touching.

After that, he stopped wanting the house. He stopped wanting the car, the name, the respectful voices. He wanted only to close the door he had found.

He decided he would do it himself. One night, alone, he would walk into the laboratory and burn every record, smash every drive, scrub out every measurement—beginning with his own. He would unfind the eleven hours.

The men who owned Project Glacier could not allow that. You do not let a man unlearn the most valuable secret in the kingdom. So they sent professionals—quiet, competent—to make Muatti's heart simply stop. A natural death for an unnatural discovery.

They had not counted on the alleys. They had not counted on the boy who could not be put down.

---

The coffee was still cold on the table. The mercenary on the floor had stopped begging and started praying, which was worse.

Muatti looked down at him with eyes gone soft and far away. He was going to bleed to death in this room, in all likelihood, long before anyone with news of him arrived. He understood that now with the same calm he understood everything else.

But it did not matter the way it should have. Before the killers came, he had done one small, stubborn thing. He had trusted neither the locks nor the drives nor the men in this building. He had written the truth out by hand—the eleven hours, the door, the calling beneath the mountain—and he had set it somewhere it could run downhill, the way water runs, the way truth runs in a city like this. Down past the white walls. Down into the dark, where the forgotten people lived and listened and never forgot a single thing.

Let them bury him, then. The secret was already loose in the under-city, looking for someone brave enough—or foolish enough—to drag it back up into the light.

Somewhere far below the floor, the walls gave their small, familiar shudder. The lamp dimmed. The air grew thick and heavy, and for half a second the dead man's knife seemed to catch a light that wasn't in the room.

Eleven hours, on the hour.

Muatti closed his eyes and let the lullaby carry him; and underneath it—faint and patient and older than the city itself—he could have sworn he heard the mountain answer back.
