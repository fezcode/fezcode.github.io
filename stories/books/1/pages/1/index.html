The walls have eyes
History is written by survivors

The acid rain came down in sheets, washing the grime from the cobblestones into the sewers, a baptism for a city that had sold its soul for a few more drops of industry. My head throbbed in time with the flickering neon sign outside my office window. The sign said "Corrigan Investigations." The bottle of bourbon on my desk said "Old Grand-Dad." Both were liars. I hadn't investigated much of anything lately, and there was nothing grand about my life.

The call came from a public phone just before midnight. Some rookie cop, his voice trembling like a leaf in a hurricane. A John Doe, face-down in an alley off 4th street. My beat. My problem.

When the coroner, a stooped old man with spectacles thick as ale bottles, rolled the body over, the city's hum faded. The face staring up at the smog-choked sky was my own. Same busted nose from that union dispute, same cynical sneer, same fresh scar above my left eye where a dame had gotten lucky with a whiskey glass last week. This wasn't just a look-alike. This was me.

"You know him, Corrigan?" the rookie asked, his awe mixing with fear.

"He's a ghost," I rasped, lighting a cigarette that tasted like ash and dread. "Let me work"

I took the case. Who else could? I was hunting my own killer.

The clues were a breadcrumb trail leading straight into my own fractured past. A matchbook from "The Tipsy Lizard," a dive bar where I'd been thrown out a month ago. A claim-ticket for my own tweed suit. A single, blurry photograph of a woman I'd loved and lost, a memory I kept buried under a mountain of bad debts and worse decisions.

I was chasing a ghost, and the closer I got, the more he felt like my own. I'd wake up in my apartment, my knuckles bruised and no memory of a fight. I'd find notes in my own handwriting, cryptic warnings I didn't remember writing. "He's getting closer," one said. "He knows what you did."

The city became a hall of mirrors, and every reflection was the dead man. I saw him in shop windows, a phantom behind my own shoulder. I saw him in the rearview mirror of my beat-up Impala. The folk I questioned—bartenders, informants, loan-sharks—they were all describing me. My habits. My temper. My sorrow.

The final clue was a key, hanging on a nail in my own office. A key to a room at the St. Marlowe hotel, a flophouse for has-beens and never-weres. I knew the room. It was the one I'd been renting for weeks, for the nights when my own apartment felt too much like a tomb. The place I'd gone after she died.

I went in, my .38 revolver cold and heavy in my hand. The room was empty, save for a single chair facing a full-length mirror propped against the wall. On the floor was a bottle of bourbon and a glass. My brand.

And I understood.

There was no doppelganger. No conspiracy. Just a man so broken by grief he'd split in two. He'd been leaving a trail for himself, a desperate attempt by one half of his mind to make the other half see the truth. The body in the alley—was it real? A stranger I'd killed in a blackout? Or just the first, most powerful delusion? It didn't matter. The case was the same. A man was hunting the part of himself he could no longer live with. The part that lost her.

I stood before the mirror. I saw him there. The killer I'd been hunting. Haggard. Haunted. His eyes full of the same self-hatred that burned in my own gut.

I had finally cornered my man. There was no escape. Not for him. Not for me.

I raised the revolver. The man in the glass did the same.

"Case closed," I whispered.
